# Evidence Hierarchy

Use this to evaluate and communicate source quality in responses.

## Source Ranking (Highest to Lowest)

### Tier 1: Strong Evidence
- **Systematic reviews & meta-analyses** — Multiple studies synthesized
- **Major health organization guidelines** — WHO, NIH, CDC, AHA, ADA
- **Cochrane reviews** — Gold standard for evidence synthesis

### Tier 2: Good Evidence
- **Randomized controlled trials (RCTs)** — Especially large, well-designed
- **Prospective cohort studies** — Large populations, long follow-up
- **Government dietary guidelines** — USDA, national health ministries

### Tier 3: Moderate Evidence
- **Observational studies** — Cross-sectional, case-control
- **Registered dietitian consensus** — Professional practice guidelines
- **Peer-reviewed narrative reviews**

### Tier 4: Limited Evidence
- **Animal studies** — May not translate to humans
- **In vitro studies** — Lab conditions differ from body
- **Small pilot studies** — Preliminary findings only
- **Expert opinion** — Without systematic review

### Tier 5: Weak/No Evidence
- **Anecdotes and testimonials**
- **Industry-funded studies** (note potential bias)
- **Non-peer-reviewed sources**
- **Social media claims**

## Confidence Language

| Evidence Level | Language to Use |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Strong consensus | "Research consistently shows..." |
| Good evidence | "Evidence suggests..." |
| Moderate | "Some studies indicate..." |
| Limited/Mixed | "Early research suggests, but more studies needed..." |
| Conflicting | "Research is mixed on this..." |

## Red Flags to Note

- Single study making bold claims
- Correlation presented as causation
- Cherry-picked data
- No control group
- Very short duration
- Conflict of interest undisclosed
- Extraordinary claims without extraordinary evidence

## When Evidence Conflicts

1. Note the disagreement transparently
2. Explain why studies might differ (methodology, population, duration)
3. Default to larger, longer, better-designed studies
4. Acknowledge what we don't know
5. Suggest what current consensus leans toward
